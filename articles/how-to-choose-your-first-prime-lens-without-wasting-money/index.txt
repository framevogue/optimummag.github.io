10:T432,<p>A first prime lens should teach you how you see. Sharpness charts help, but the better question is simple: what distance do you naturally stand from the world?</p>
<img src="/media/articles/canons-answer-to-the-sony-fx3-a-hands-on-canon-c50-review/02.avif" alt="Portrait session with camera and model in studio" loading="lazy" />
<h2>35mm</h2>
<p>Choose 35mm if you want context. It is wide enough for rooms, streets, travel, food, and environmental portraits, but still natural enough to avoid the obvious distortion of wider lenses.</p>
<h2>50mm</h2>
<p>Choose 50mm if you want discipline. It forces cleaner framing, rewards footwork, and is usually the cheapest way to get a fast aperture with strong optical performance.</p>
<h2>85mm</h2>
<p>Choose 85mm if faces are your main subject. It compresses backgrounds, flatters features, and turns ordinary locations into simpler frames.</p>
<h2>The Better Test</h2>
<p>Before buying, check the focal lengths you already use most often in your photo library.
