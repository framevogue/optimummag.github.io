3:T439,<p>You do not need a large studio to make controlled portraits. You need one window, a little distance, and the patience to watch how light falls across a face.</p>
<img src="/media/articles/canons-answer-to-the-sony-fx3-a-hands-on-canon-c50-review/03.avif" alt="Studio portrait subject under soft light" loading="lazy" />
<h2>Place The Window To The Side</h2>
<p>Start with the subject about forty-five degrees from the window. This gives shape to the face without making the shadow side collapse into darkness.</p>
<h2>Add Negative Fill</h2>
<p>A dark coat, black foam board, or the unlit side of a room can deepen contrast. Negative fill is not about making the image darker; it is about giving the light edge and direction.</p>
<h2>Move The Background</h2>
<p>Pull the subject away from the wall when possible. Even a meter of separation lets the background soften and makes the portrait feel more intentional.</p>
<h2>Keep The Frame Honest</h2>
<p>Use a 50mm or 85mm lens, expose for the skin, and avoid rescuing every shadow in post.
