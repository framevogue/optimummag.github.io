<p>The Sony a6400 occupies an interesting place in the mirrorless camera landscape — it&#039;s small enough to carry everywhere, yet its autofocus system rivals cameras costing significantly more. We tested it shooting a wedding, studio portraits, and daily family life over two weeks to see where it shines and where it stumbles.</p>
<hr />
<h2>Key Specs</h2>
<ul>
<li>24.2 MP APS-C sensor</li>
<li>425 phase-detection + 425 contrast-detection AF points</li>
<li>Real-time Eye AF (always active)</li>
<li>11 fps continuous shooting with AF tracking</li>
<li>4K video (no crop)</li>
<li>180° tilting touchscreen</li>
<li>Weather-sealed body</li>
<li>0.02-second AF acquisition</li>
</ul>
<hr />
<h2>Autofocus — The Main Event</h2>
<p>Sony&#039;s AF system is light years ahead of most competitors. Real-time Eye AF is always active — the camera continuously locks onto eyes without you needing to press a separate button. Real-time tracking follows subjects tenaciously across the frame. For a camera at this price point, the AF performance is genuinely remarkable.</p>
<hr />
<h2>Where It Struggles</h2>
<p><strong>Low light:</strong> ISO above 2000 introduces noticeable noise. The dynamic range, while decent, can&#039;t match full-frame bodies. If you shoot weddings or indoor events frequently, this camera will feel limiting.</p>
<p><strong>Touchscreen:</strong> Calling it a &quot;touchscreen&quot; is generous. Responsiveness is slow, and functionality is limited. Don&#039;t expect iPhone-level interaction.</p>
<p><strong>Menu system:</strong> Sony&#039;s menu remains overwhelming. You&#039;ll need to set up the custom menu to make daily operation bearable.</p>
<p><strong>No IBIS:</strong> The lack of in-body stabilization is a real drawback for video shooters and low-light stills work.</p>
<hr />
<h2>Image Quality</h2>
<p>With fast glass, the a6400 produces excellent results. The kit lens (16-50mm f/3.5-5.6) is serviceable but limiting — invest in a prime like the Sony 35mm f/1.8 OSS or the compact FE 28mm f/2 to unlock the camera&#039;s potential. Colors are pleasing, and 24 megapixels provides ample detail for prints and cropping.</p>
<hr />
<h2>Who Should Buy It</h2>
<p>This is an ideal travel, family, and everyday camera — small enough to throw in a bag, with autofocus that can keep up with kids and candid moments. For professional work (especially weddings or low-light events), we recommend stepping up to a full-frame Sony body.</p>
<hr />
<h2>Practical Tips</h2>
<ul>
<li>Set up the custom My Menu immediately — it will save you hours of frustration navigating Sony&#039;s default interface.</li>
<li>Invest in a fast prime lens. The body is capable of much more than the kit lens reveals.</li>
<li>For video, consider a small external mic — the flip-up screen is blocked by most hot-shoe microphones without a rig.</li>
</ul>
<hr />
<h2>FAQ</h2>
<p><strong>Q: Is the Sony a6400 good for video?</strong> It shoots excellent 4K video with no crop, but lacks in-body stabilization and a headphone jack. A gimbal or stabilized lens is recommended for handheld work.</p>
<p><strong>Q: Does the a6400 have weather sealing?</strong> Yes — it&#039;s weather-sealed against dust and moisture, though not fully waterproof.</p>
<p><strong>Q: What lenses should I buy with the Sony a6400?</strong> The Sony 35mm f/1.8 OSS is a great all-around prime.
